#### Abstract
Controlling charge and energy flow at the nanoscale is central to quantum materials, optoelectronics, and sensing, yet these processes occur on ultrafast timescales and involve complex quantum interactions. In this talk, I will present my work on using light-matter interactions to direct charge and energy flow in nanoscale quantum systems. I will show how intrinsic dipole moments in polar two-dimensional materials enhance charge transfer and nonlinear optical responses, how engineered phonon interference in two-dimensional metals enables molecular sensing, and how ultrafast multidimensional spectroscopy can probe energy transport in complex systems. Together, these examples illustrate how advanced spectroscopic tools can uncover new mechanisms for controlling charge and energy transport, with implications ranging from next-generation photonic devices to quantum-enabled sensors.
