#### Abstract
Experiments in quantum optics and photonics over the past decade have demonstrated key working principles for the next generation of light-based quantum technologies for sensing, computing, and communication—technologies that could significantly outperform existing classical approaches in speed, sensitivity, and efficiency. However, despite rapid progress, achieving practical quantum advantage will require generating large-scale quantum states of light involving far more photons than demonstrated so far(~100). In addition to optical loss, current limitations arise primarily from the low efficiency of existing methods for generating and manipulating of quantum light, which is fundamentally constrained by the weak interaction between photons and matter.

In this talk, two recently developed platforms will be discussed that may help overcome these limitations and enable scalable quantum photonics. First, a newly developed method of avalanche-enhanced all-optical modulation will be presented, which enables modulation at single-photon intensities and demonstrates an extremely large effective nonlinear refractive index—several orders of magnitude higher than that of standard nonlinear materials. Second, solid-state single-photon emitters coupled to plasmonic structures will be considered, which provide ultra-tight electromagnetic field confinement and dramatically enhances light–matter interaction. Together, these developments point toward the possibility of building ultrafast all-optical circuits that may operate at sub-THz to THz frequencies, offering a route to large-scale quantum photonic systems.

#### Biography
Demid Sychev is a postdoctoral researcher at Purdue University, specializing in quantum optics, photonics with solid-state quantum emitters, and nonlinear optics. From 2015 to 2020, he conducted doctoral research under the supervision of Professor Alex Lvovsky at the Russian Quantum Center, working on the generation of non-classical states of light, such as Schrödinger’s cat states and NOON states, as well as methods for creating quantum entanglement and teleportation of optical qubits for quantum information applications. Since 2020, he has been working in Professor Vladimir Shalaev's group at Purdue University, where he focuses on plasmon-enhanced solid-state single-photon emitters and methods for achieving optical nonlinearity at the single-photon intensity levels.
